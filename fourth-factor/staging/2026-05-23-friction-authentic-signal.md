# [UNREVIEWED] Friction-Authentic Signal — A New Frame for `characteristic_unexpectedness`
*Staged: 2026-05-23. Low bandwidth session (Jay at 1/10). Review before promoting to architecture.md.*

---

## THE INSIGHT SOURCE

Two sideways thoughts collided:
1. "If Tony came back from the dead and had to re-auth into JARVIS" — the 'omfg I'm standing right here, let me in' moment
2. Rogers IVR system — screaming at the IVR got you escalated to L3 tech

Both point at the same thing: **friction-generated behavior is higher signal than unprompted behavior.**

---

## THE FRAME

Current architecture defines `characteristic_unexpectedness` as: "surprising in a way that matches the operator's characteristic surprise signature."

This describes the output. The Tony insight points at the generator.

**Proposed reframe:** `characteristic_unexpectedness` is what the operator produces when the system resists them.

Not "unexpected behavior" — **friction-generated behavior.** The signal isn't in the surprise. It's in how they push back when blocked.

---

## WHY THIS MATTERS

Tony screaming at JARVIS is behaviorally richer than Tony calmly logging in. Because:
- Friction forces authentic generation
- You cannot precompute your frustration response to a novel obstacle
- You can only generate it from your actual wiring
- An impersonator with the profile can mimic calm Tony
- They cannot mimic frustrated Tony — because frustrated Tony is generated by Tony's specific combination of ego, urgency, pattern recognition, and vocabulary under pressure

Rogers IVR: screaming got escalation not because system detected emotion, but because the *specific pattern* of frustration matched what a real customer in genuine distress produces. An attacker learns "be angry." They cannot fake the texture of "I've been explaining this for 20 minutes" because that texture requires actually having been doing it.

---

## THE MATH IMPLICATION

`characteristic_unexpectedness` may not be a separate variable. It may be a **multiplier on friction events.** When the system creates resistance — error injection, wrong premise, deliberate friction — the response is weighted higher in the confidence function because:
- Friction-generated behavior has higher entropy-per-character than unprompted behavior
- It's harder to fake
- It's more operator-specific

Proposed formula addition:

```
CC(R, B) = w1·reframing_depth(R)
         + w2·reframe_sequence(R)
         + w3·error_injection_response(R)
         + w4·characteristic_unexpectedness(R, B)  ← now friction-weighted
         - w5·smoothness_penalty(R, B)
         + w6·friction_event_response(R, B)         ← NEW
```

Where `friction_event_response` captures: what does this operator generate when the system resists them? How characteristic is that generation? Does it match the enrolled manifold?

---

## THE KEY DISTINCTION — operator friction vs chaos injection

A bot injecting chaos produces *random* surprise — flat distribution.
An operator under friction produces *characteristic* surprise — distribution skewed toward their specific wiring.

The manifold doesn't just describe what they do. It describes what they do **when things go wrong.** That's harder to fake and higher signal.

**The smoothness penalty (already in formula) may be the chaos rejection mechanism.** Smooth = low signal = low score. Characteristic friction = high signal = high score. But this needs explicit definition in M2.

## OPEN QUESTIONS FOR REVIEW

1. Is `friction_event_response` genuinely new, or is it already captured by error_injection_response (Type 02)? If so, does Type 02 need to be broadened?
2. Is `characteristic_unexpectedness` a multiplier, a standalone variable, or the cognitive confidence number itself (as originally suspected)?
3. Does the staging/challenge generator (from red team finding #5) connect here? If the system can generate friction deliberately, does that make this higher signal than naturally-occurring friction?
4. Rogers L3 escalation: the system didn't detect anger — it detected a *pattern* that matched real distress. Is there a parallel metric here for pattern-vs-performance distinction?
